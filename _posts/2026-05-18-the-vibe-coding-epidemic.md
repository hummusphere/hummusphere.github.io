    <h3 class="blog-sub-header"> It's Making Me Stupider</h3> 
    <p> Recently, I have been creating my own <a class="blog-link" href="https://github.com/hummusphere/hummOS"> operating system<svg class="blog-external" width="15" height="15" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg"><path d="M10 6H6C4.89543 6 4 6.89543 4 8V18C4 19.1046 4.89543 20 6 20H16C17.1046 20 18 19.1046 18 18V14M14 4H20M20 4V10M20 4L10 14" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"></path></svg></a>. It's nothing fancy, just a simple bootloader and kernel made out of <code>assembly</code>. It's essentially command-line interface that can run some games and other cool tricks. The entire thing probably took me around 40 hours. I am thrilled with how it turned out, and I learned assembly throughout the process. However, this project was a little different for me. It was the first time I used AI to help me create it. In my humble opinion, it's making me stupider.</p>
    <img class="blog-image" src="https://i.redd.it/kr1ymgu7qp9g1.png" style="width:100%; max-width: 480px;">
    <h3 class="blog-sub-header"> How I Used It </h3> 
    <p>The main thing I used AI for was finding bugs. When I didn't understand how my code was working, I would just plop it into <a class="blog-link" href="https://openai.com/index/chatgpt/">ChatGPT<svg class="blog-external" width="15" height="15" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg"><path d="M10 6H6C4.89543 6 4 6.89543 4 8V18C4 19.1046 4.89543 20 6 20H16C17.1046 20 18 19.1046 18 18V14M14 4H20M20 4V10M20 4L10 14" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"></path></svg></a> and ask it what the bug was. It could spot any problem almost immediately. However, looking back, I realized that it was just making me lazy. By getting an AI to find bugs for me, I wasn't training my brain. In addition, looking for bugs manually helps you understand the program you are working with better. Besides, it just promotes sloppy coding. If ChatGPT can find the bugs for you instantly, then why bother writing good code if it can just fix it for you later? I feel like my understanding of assembly is not as strong as it could have been had I not used any AI.</p>
    <h3 class="blog-sub-header"> Moving Forward </h3> 
    <p> We need to rethink how we use AI. There are some things that AI is really good for. For example, finding commands or how to install dependencies. Before, you had to search the entire internet and read multiple forums to find the right commands to install certain dependencies. I was able to easily install <a class="blog-link" href="https://www.gnu.org/software/grub/">GRUB<svg class="blog-external" width="15" height="15" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg"><path d="M10 6H6C4.89543 6 4 6.89543 4 8V18C4 19.1046 4.89543 20 6 20H16C17.1046 20 18 19.1046 18 18V14M14 4H20M20 4V10M20 4L10 14" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"></path></svg></a> onto my flash drive with instructions from ChatGPT. This process probably would have taken longer if I had to search the internet to find instructions. I definitely saved time here, and I didn't lose anything in return. However, by asking ChatGPT to find bugs for me, I am losing my ability to debug by myself and have a weaker understanding of the system I am creating. Therefore, in the future, I am not going to use any AI to debug. I am just going to use my brain to try and understand what is going wrong. I may spend more time on a project, but in the end I will have a better understanding of how it works.</p>
